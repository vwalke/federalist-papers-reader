# Product

## Register

brand

## Platform

web

## Users

The primary users are curious adult readers approaching the Federalist Papers as interested citizens, family members, or friends rather than as scholars. They may read one paper each week on a phone, tablet, or computer and want help seeing the argument without feeling as if they have opened an academic database.

## Product Purpose

Publius makes all 85 Federalist Papers inviting to read together. It preserves the original words, places them in an atmospheric but accessible newspaper setting, offers an optional clean reader, and supplies just enough context to help a lay reader follow the argument. Success means readers want to continue to the next paper and can comfortably do so on any screen.

## Positioning

The argument as it met its first readers—made comfortable for readers today.

## Conversion & proof

- Primary action: continue with the first unread paper.
- Secondary action: browse the complete index by number, author, date, or message.
- The line a visitor should remember: “Read the debate in the form it first entered public life.”
- Belief ladder: these texts were public newspaper arguments; their original wording is worth preserving; period atmosphere can make them more vivid; thoughtful guidance can make them less intimidating; the complete collection is approachable one paper at a time.
- Proof on hand: the complete public-domain text and publication history are grounded in the Library of Congress, Founders Online, and the historic newspaper record documented in `docs/sources.md`.

## Brand Personality

Tactile, inviting, and intelligent. The voice is warm and plainspoken, treats readers as capable adults, and favors curiosity over instruction. The emotional goal is the quiet excitement of unfolding a consequential newspaper at a shared kitchen table.

## Anti-references

The site must not resemble a modern SaaS dashboard, a patriotic campaign site, a beige lifestyle brand, an academic research database, or a literal damaged facsimile that asks readers to decipher stains, tiny columns, and obsolete letterforms. It must not turn eighteenth-century atmosphere into colonial costume or decorative clutter.

## Design Principles

1. Make history tactile without making it hard to read.
2. Let the original argument remain the main event.
3. Offer context as a companion, never as a lecture.
4. Compose for the reader’s actual screen instead of shrinking a broadsheet.
5. Reward steady progress without requiring an account.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

The complete reading experience must work with keyboard navigation, screen readers, browser text enlargement, reduced motion, and high-contrast text. Gazette mode becomes a true single-column broadside on phones. Color never carries meaning alone, touch targets are at least 44 by 44 CSS pixels, and JavaScript or browser-storage failure never hides the essays or core navigation.
