# Charles Thomson About-page copy design

**Date:** July 15, 2026  
**Status:** Approved direction; wording revised from user feedback

## Goal

Replace the restrained Charles Thomson passage on the About page with a warm, inspiring three-paragraph mini-profile. It should help a general reader—including a curious twelve-year-old—understand why Thomson's life and work were remarkable, while keeping the family connection at the center.

## Narrative shape

The section will follow a simple rise-and-legacy arc:

1. Begin with the family relationship and the vivid adversity Thomson faced at ten years old.
2. Explain why his name on the first published Declaration mattered, without diminishing his role by contrasting it with the later parchment signatures.
3. Gather his other achievements into a memorable finish and return to the experience of reading with Mom.

The tone should be proud, direct, and accessible rather than academic. Historical links will be attached unobtrusively to the claims they support; there will be no footnote block.

## Approved copy direction

> The reading has another personal connection. Charles Thomson was my sixth-great-uncle—my mom's fifth-great-uncle. At ten years old, he arrived in America with no parents and no money. His mother had died in Ireland; his father died during the Atlantic crossing, and the brothers were separated after they landed. From that beginning, Thomson rose to become the only Secretary of the Continental Congress, serving from its first meeting in 1774 until the new federal government began in 1789.
>
> Thomson's and John Hancock's were the two names printed on the Dunlap broadside—the Declaration of Independence's first published form, dispatched throughout the states and to Washington's army before the famous parchment was engrossed and signed. The parchment became the enduring national icon; Thomson's attestation appeared on the version that first carried independence into American public life.
>
> And that was only one chapter. Thomson helped bring the Great Seal of the United States to its final design, personally carried word of George Washington's unanimous election to Mount Vernon, and later spent nearly twenty years translating the Septuagint Bible from Greek into English—the first published English translation of the ancient Greek Old Testament. The penniless ten-year-old orphan grew into a man who helped give the new nation its public words, its emblem, and its institutional memory. That is a remarkable family thread for my mom and me to feel as we read these arguments together.

Minor copyediting for rhythm is allowed during implementation, but the plainspoken opening, the phrase “Septuagint Bible,” the Greek-to-English explanation, and the affirmative treatment of the Declaration role must remain.

## Historical support and linking

- Link the childhood claim to [Carpenters' Hall](https://www.carpentershall.org/pages/my-zeal-for-liberty), which describes Thomson as a penniless immigrant child and recounts his father's death, the loss of the family's money, and the children's separation.
- Link the congressional-service claim to the [Library of Congress](https://www.loc.gov/collections/continental-congress-and-constitutional-convention-from-1774-to-1789/articles-and-essays/to-form-a-more-perfect-union/charles-thomson/).
- Link the Dunlap broadside claim to the [National Archives](https://www.archives.gov/founding-docs/declaration-history).
- Link the Great Seal claim to the [National Museum of American Diplomacy](https://diplomacy.state.gov/the-great-seal/).
- Link the Washington claim to Thomson's notification address at [Founders Online](https://founders.archives.gov/documents/Washington/05-02-02-0056).
- Link the Bible claim to the [Museum of the Bible](https://collections.museumofthebible.org/artifacts/45021-thomson-bible?theme=a-history-of-translation), with the original 1808 title page available through [Wikisource](https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_%28Thomson%29) if useful.

Do not say that Congress sent the Declaration directly to King George III. The Dunlap broadside reached Britain through British channels, but the National Park Service states that Congress did not send a copy to the king.

## Presentation

Keep the existing heading, typography, spacing, and overall About-page layout. This is a copy and citation improvement, not a visual redesign. The links should inherit the About page's established inline-link treatment.
