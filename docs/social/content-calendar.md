# 2-Week Starter Calendar — Anchor Posts

Fourteen finished, ready-to-approve evergreen posts — **two weeks of daily
content**. These are the **backbone**. On any given day, if the news offers a
stronger hook, swap the evergreen post for a same-day news-hook (see
`strategy.md`) and push the evergreen down the queue — nothing here expires.

**Two-part format (non-Premium reach fix):** post the **main text + card image
first with no link**, then reply to your own post with the link. A link in a
non-Premium main post gets buried; a link in the first reply does not.

- Posted **manually by Vann** in the X app (no tool, no API).
- Main post carries the card `social-cards/{n}.jpg` (attach unless noted).
- Link `https://federalistreader.org/papers/{n}` goes in the **first reply**.
- Post mid-morning ET. Replies/reposts happen natively on X too.

### Day 1 — Fact
> **Post:** All 85 Federalist Papers were signed "Publius." Publius was actually
> three men: Hamilton, Madison, and Jay, writing as one. They kept it quiet so
> well that historians still argue over who wrote about a dozen of the essays.
>
> **↳ First reply:** Start at the beginning: federalistreader.org/papers/1

`card: 1.jpg` (attach to the main post)

### Day 2 — Quote
> **Post:** "If men were angels, no government would be necessary." Madison wrote
> that in Federalist 51. Since people aren't angels, he argued, the trick is
> building a government that also controls itself. It's the whole idea behind
> checks and balances.
>
> **↳ First reply:** Read Federalist 51: federalistreader.org/papers/51

`card: 51.jpg`

### Day 3 — Question
> **Post:** Madison's fix for faction in Federalist 10 was counterintuitive.
> Don't try to stamp it out. Make the republic big enough that no single faction
> can take over. Does that still hold at 330 million people?
>
> **↳ First reply:** His full argument: federalistreader.org/papers/10

`card: 10.jpg`

### Day 4 — Myth-buster
> **Post:** The founders did not all agree, and the Federalist Papers are the
> proof. They were written because ratification was genuinely in doubt, especially
> in New York, and Publius was making the case to skeptical readers one newspaper
> essay at a time.
>
> **↳ First reply:** How it started: federalistreader.org/papers/1

`card: 1.jpg`

### Day 5 — Fact (counter-intuitive)
> **Post:** In Federalist 84, Hamilton argued against adding a Bill of Rights. He
> worried that writing down some rights would imply the ones you left off weren't
> protected. He lost that fight, but the reasoning is worth reading.
>
> **↳ First reply:** Read it here: federalistreader.org/papers/84

`card: 84.jpg`

### Day 6 — Quote
> **Post:** "Energy in the executive is a leading character in the definition of
> good government." Hamilton wrote that in Federalist 70, making the case for one
> president instead of a committee. One person, he argued, is easier to watch and
> hold responsible.
>
> **↳ First reply:** Federalist 70: federalistreader.org/papers/70

`card: 70.jpg`

### Day 7 — Question
> **Post:** Hamilton called the courts the "least dangerous" branch in Federalist
> 78. They command neither the army nor the budget, only judgment. Two centuries
> later, does that still describe the judiciary?
>
> **↳ First reply:** His argument: federalistreader.org/papers/78

`card: 78.jpg`

### Day 8 — On this day / anniversary
> **Post:** On July 26, 1788, New York voted to ratify the Constitution. That
> vote was the entire point of the Federalist Papers. All 85 essays were aimed at
> persuading New York's readers, and it worked.
>
> **↳ First reply:** The essay that closed the case: federalistreader.org/papers/85

`card: 85.jpg`

### Day 9 — Fact
> **Post:** The Electoral College was built for reasons most people never hear
> about. In Federalist 68, Hamilton describes it as a guard against demagogues and
> against foreign powers helping to pick an American president.
>
> **↳ First reply:** The original rationale: federalistreader.org/papers/68

`card: 68.jpg`

### Day 10 — Quote
> **Post:** Piling all the powers "in the same hands," Madison warned in
> Federalist 47, "may justly be pronounced the very definition of tyranny." That
> is the reason the framers split the government into three branches.
>
> **↳ First reply:** Read Federalist 47: federalistreader.org/papers/47

`card: 47.jpg`

### Day 11 — Question
> **Post:** Madison in Federalist 45 called federal powers "few and defined" and
> the powers left to the states "numerous and indefinite." Is that still a fair
> description of the balance today?
>
> **↳ First reply:** See how he drew the line: federalistreader.org/papers/45

`card: 45.jpg`

### Day 12 — Fact
> **Post:** For more than 200 years, scholars have argued over who really wrote
> about a dozen of the Federalist Papers, Madison or Hamilton. Both men later
> claimed some of the same essays.
>
> **↳ First reply:** Read the disputed ones and pick a side:
> federalistreader.org/papers/51

`card: 51.jpg`

### Day 13 — Quote
> **Post:** The very first Federalist essay sets the stakes for all of them. Can a
> people build good government "from reflection and choice," Hamilton asks, or are
> they "forever destined to depend for their political constitutions on accident
> and force"?
>
> **↳ First reply:** Where it begins: federalistreader.org/papers/1

`card: 1.jpg`

### Day 14 — Observation / myth-buster
> **Post:** Critics in 1788 said the new presidency looked too much like a
> monarchy. Hamilton answered them in Federalist 69, going down the list and
> comparing the president to the British king power by power. The differences add
> up.
>
> **↳ First reply:** Read Federalist 69: federalistreader.org/papers/69
